Definition

What is Requirements Gathering?

A crucial phase in the SDLC that involves collecting, documenting, and managing requirements that define the features and functionalities of a system. The success of a project often depends on the accuracy and completeness of gathered requirements.

Main Subprocesses (5)

1. Stakeholder Identification

Identify all stakeholders affected by the system, directly or indirectly. Done via interviews, surveys, or workshops.

2. Stakeholder Analysis

Understand the needs, expectations, and influence of each stakeholder. Prioritize requirements and manage conflicting interests.

3. Problem Definition

Clearly define the problems or opportunities the software aims to address. Engage stakeholders in discussions.

4. Requirements Documentation

Document gathered requirements using requirements documents, use cases, user stories, or prototypes.

5. Validation & Verification

Ensure requirements are accurate, complete, and consistent. Conduct reviews, walkthroughs, or use validation tools.

6-Step Process
  • Step 1 – Assign Roles: Identify and engage all relevant stakeholders (end-users, clients, PMs, SMEs).
  • Step 2 – Define Project Scope: Outline objectives, boundaries, and limitations.
  • Step 3 – Conduct Stakeholder Interviews: Use open-ended questions to uncover explicit and implicit requirements.
  • Step 4 – Document Requirements: Write user stories, use cases, or formal specs including functional and non-functional requirements.
  • Step 5 – Verify & Validate: Verification aligns with stakeholders' intentions; Validation ensures project goals are met.
  • Step 6 – Prioritize Requirements: Based on project goals and constraints to guide development roadmap.
7 Gathering Techniques
1

Interviews

One-on-one or group sessions with stakeholders for detailed information.

2

Surveys & Questionnaires

Distributing forms to a broad audience for large-scale data collection.

3

Workshops

Facilitated group sessions for collaboration and conflict resolution.

4

Observation

Directly watching end-users to uncover implicit requirements.

5

Prototyping

Creating mockups for early visualization and stakeholder feedback.

6

Use Cases & Scenarios

Describing system-user interactions to identify functional requirements.

7

Document Analysis

Reviewing existing docs (manuals, reports) to extract relevant information.

Why It's Important (5 Reasons)
  • Clarity of Objectives – Shared understanding of what needs to be achieved
  • Customer Satisfaction – Product aligns with user desires
  • Scope Definition – Prevents scope creep, manages expectations
  • Reduced Misunderstandings – Clear communication prevents ambiguity
  • Risk Mitigation – Identifies issues early before they become costly
6 Benefits

Cost Reduction

Well-defined requirements minimize costly changes and rework later.

Customer Satisfaction

Product closely matches stakeholder expectations; builds trust.

Efficient Resource Utilization

Accurate allocation of time, manpower, and technology.

Enhanced Quality

Clear requirements enable effective testing strategies.

Risk Management

Identifies potential risks and ambiguities early on.

Accurate Planning

Realistic schedules, milestones, and deliverables.

7 Common Obstacles
  • Unclear Objectives – Leads to confusion and scope creep
  • Ambiguous Requirements – Vague language causes misunderstandings
  • Poor Stakeholder Involvement – Critical requirements get missed
  • Changing Requirements (Scope Creep) – Causes delays and increased costs
  • Communication Barriers – Language gaps and misinterpretations
  • Overreliance on Documentation – Misses evolving needs without active collaboration
  • Lack of User Involvement – Results in low usability and acceptance
Requirements Gathering in Agile (8 Concepts)

User Stories

Format: "As a [user], I want [action] so that [benefit]." Captures functionality from user perspective.

Backlog Refinement

Regular sessions (grooming) to review, clarify, and prioritize backlog items.

Iterative Development

Work organized into time-boxed sprints with continuous refinement.

Continuous Stakeholder Collaboration

Sprint reviews and planning meetings for ongoing feedback.

Prototyping & Visual Aids

Wireframes and prototypes for early feedback and alignment.

Daily Stand-ups

Brief meetings for progress updates and alignment. Also known as daily scrums.

Acceptance Criteria

Conditions that must be met for a user story to be considered complete.

Retrospectives

End-of-sprint reflections to improve future processes.

Agile Challenges (5)
  • Changing Priorities – Balance flexibility with stability
  • Balancing Detail & Flexibility – Enough detail to guide dev, flexible enough to adapt
  • Effective Communication – Open channels prevent misunderstandings
  • Overemphasis on Documentation – Maintain minimal but effective docs
  • Ensuring Continuous Feedback – Active stakeholder involvement can be challenging

Functional Decomposition Diagram

What is it?

A system design and analysis method that involves breaking down a complex system into smaller, more manageable components. It identifies functions and defines how they interact to achieve the system's objectives.

  • Goal: Simplify complex systems into understandable parts
  • Makes it easy to design, analyze, and implement complex systems
  • Breaks down using specific functions and relationships
Use Case Diagram

Definition

A type of UML (Unified Modeling Language) diagram that represents the interaction between actors and a system to accomplish specific goals. Provides a high-level view of the system's functionality.

3 Key Elements of Use Case Diagrams

👤 Actors

External entities (users, other systems, hardware) that interact with the system. They initiate use cases and receive outcomes. Proper identification is crucial for modeling system behavior.

⭕ Use Cases

Represent specific things the system can do (e.g., "Place Order," "Track Delivery"). Represented by ovals.

⬜ System Boundary

A rectangular box that defines what is inside vs. outside the system. Establishes clear scope of the system being modeled.

4 Use Case Diagram Relationships
Association

Communication between an actor and a use case. Depicted by a plain line connecting them.

Include

A use case includes the functionality of another. Dashed arrow pointing from including → included. Promotes reusable design.

Extend

A use case can be extended under specific conditions. Dashed arrow with keyword "extend." Handles optional/exceptional behavior.

Generalization

An "is-a" relationship between use cases. Arrow points from specialized → general use case.

Data Flow Diagram (DFD)

Definition

Represents the flow of data in a system or process. Shows inputs and outputs of each entity and process. Key facts:

  • No control flow, no loops or decision rules
  • Graphical tool useful for communicating with users, managers, and personnel
  • Useful for analyzing both existing and proposed systems
2 Types of DFD

Logical DFD

Focuses on the system process and how data flows. High-level without diving into technical implementation. Used for smooth running of systems (e.g., Banking software).

Physical DFD

Shows how data flow is actually implemented. Includes additional details like data storage, transmission, and specific technology/components. More specific and close to implementation.

3 DFD Levels
  • Level 0 (Context Diagram): Abstract view showing the entire system as a single bubble with input/output relationships to external entities.
  • Level 1 DFD: Breaks down major processes from Level 0 into sub-processes. Shows data flows and data stores for each sub-process. Highlights main functions.
  • Level 2 DFD: Even more detailed — further breaks down sub-processes from Level 1. Each sub-process becomes its own separate process with associated data flows and stores.
Quick Comparison
Diagram Purpose Key Visual
Functional Decomposition Break down system into functions Hierarchical tree
Use Case Diagram Show system-actor interactions Actors + ovals + system box
Data Flow Diagram Show data movement in system Bubbles + arrows + stores
